Measures of Variation

Measures of variation provide insights into the spread or variability within a dataset. They help us understand the consistency or diversity of data values. Common measures include the range, standard deviation, and variance. These measures are crucial in comparing datasets and determining data reliability.

Range

The range is the difference between the largest and smallest values in a dataset. It provides a very easy to calculate measure of variability. It is found by the formula:

Range = Max - Min

While range is an easy way to gauge variance in datasets, it uses outliers in it's calculation making it not as reliable as other measures of variation.

Standard Deviation

The standard deviation quantifies the average deviation of each data point from the mean \( \bar{x} \). It is the most commonly used statistic to assess variability in datasets. The standard deviation is commonly represented with \( s \) and is found by the following formula:

\[ s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}} \]

Standard deviation will have the same units as the original data. Later on in the course we will reference the population standard deviation which is commonly represented by the \( \sigma \) symbol (the greek letter "sigma").

Variance

Variance is the square of the standard deviation and provides a measure of data spread in squared units. It can be found by squaring the standard deviation. Variance for the sample is represented by \(s^2\) where the population variance is represented by \( \sigma^2 \).

Chebyshev's Rule

Chebyshev's Rule states that for any dataset, at least \( 1 - \frac{1}{k^2} \) of the data lies within \( k \) standard deviations from the mean, where \( k > 1 \).

Example: For \( k = 2 \), at least 75% of the data lies within two standard deviations of the mean.

Empirical Rule

The Empirical Rule applies to bell-shaped (normal) distributions. Approximately:

  • 68% of the data lies within 1 standard deviation of the mean.
  • 95% of the data lies within 2 standard deviations of the mean.
  • 99.7% of the data lies within 3 standard deviations of the mean.
