Measures of Location

Understanding the position of data values relative to others in a dataset helps us interpret and compare data effectively. This lesson covers four key concepts: z-scores, percentiles, quartiles, and box plots. These tools allow us to analyze individual data points in context and summarize datasets visually.

Z-Scores

Definition: Z-Score

A z-score measures how many standard deviations a sample data value is from the mean of the dataset. It is calculated using the formula:

\[ z = \frac{x - \bar{x}}{s} \]

where \( x \) is the data value, \( \bar{x} \) is the sample mean, and \( s \) is the sample standard deviation.

Important Properties of Z-Scores

  • A z-score is the number of standard deviations that a given value \( x \) is above or below the mean.
  • Z-scores are expressed as numbers with no units of measurement.
  • Data values are considered significantly low if their z-score is \( \leq -2 \) and significantly high if their z-score is \( \geq 2 \).
  • Values less than than the mean will have a negative z-score
  • Values greater than the mean will have a positive z-score.

Round-Off Rule for Z-Scores: Typically we round z-scores to two decimal places.

Example : Calculating a Z-Score

Suppose the mean test score in a class is 70 with a standard deviation of 10. A student scored 85. Calculate the z-score (ie standardized value) for this student and use that value to determine if this student's score is considered significantly high.

Solution

\[ z = \frac{85 - 70}{10} = 1.5 \]

This means the student scored 1.5 standard deviations above the mean.

Example : Working with Z-Scores

A nutritionist is studying the daily calcium intake (in mg) of a group of individuals. The daily calcium intake follows a normal distribution with a mean of 1000 mg and a standard deviation of 120 mg.

Part 1: A particular individual consumes 740 mg of calcium per day. Calculate the z-score for this intake.

Part 2: If another individual has a z-score of 1.5, determine their daily calcium intake in mg.

Solution

Part 1:

The formula for the z-score is:

$$ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} $$

Substituting the given values:

$$ z = \frac{740 - 1000}{120} $$

$$ z = \frac{-260}{120} $$

$$ z \approx -2.17 $$

This z-score is less than -2, indicating that a calcium intake of 740 mg is significantly below the average.

Part 2:

To find the data value corresponding to a z-score of 1.5, we use the rearranged z-score formula:

$$ x = \mu + z\sigma $$

Substituting the values:

$$ x = 1000 + (1.5)(120) $$

$$ x = 1000 + 180 $$

$$ x = 1180 $$

An individual with a z-score of 1.5 has a daily calcium intake of 1180 mg.

Example : Comparing Exam Scores from Different Classes

Student A scored 90 on an exam with a mean of 85 and a standard deviation of 5. Student B scored 78 on an exam with a mean of 70 and a standard deviation of 4. Which student performed better relative to their class?

Solution

For Student A:

\[ z = \frac{90 - 85}{5} = 1.00 \]

For Student B:

\[ z = \frac{78 - 70}{4} = 2.00 \]

Interpretation: Student B performed better relative to their class, as their z-score is higher.

Percentiles

Percentiles are an essential tool in statistical analysis, used to divide data into groups with equal proportions of values. They help in understanding the distribution and significance of data points, which is crucial not only for everyday data interpretation but also for advanced statistical methods like resampling.

Definition: Percentile

A percentile indicates the percentage of data values below a particular value in a dataset. For example, the 90th percentile means 90% of the data values are below the given value. We can find the percentile of a data value \( x \) using the following formula: \[ \text{Percentile of value }x=\frac{\text{number of values less than}\, x}{n}*100\] Often a percentile is represented as \( P_k \) where \( k \) is the percentile. So the 90th percentile would be \(P_{90}\).

Example : Finding a Percentile

The following data represents the daily number of customers visiting a coffee shop over the course of 10 days: 35, 10, 45, 5, 30, 25, 15, 50, 20, 40. What percentile does the data value of 40 correspond to?

5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50

Solution

We first need to make sure we order the dataset. We can use technology to order the data for later datasets. We get 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50. Then we perform the calculation, the 40th percentile lies at the 4th value (calculated as \( 0.4 \times 10 = 4 \)). Thus, the 40th percentile is 20.

Quartiles

Quartiles divide data into four equal groups, with approximately 25% of the data in each group. These are key measures of location that help in understanding the distribution of a dataset. Just as there are 99 percentiles dividing data into 100 groups, there are three quartiles dividing data into four groups.

Definition: Quartiles

Quartiles are denoted as Q1, Q2, and Q3:

  • Q1 (First Quartile): The same as the 25th percentile, separating the lowest 25% of values from the rest.
  • Q2 (Second Quartile): The same as the median or 50th percentile, dividing the dataset into two equal halves.
  • Q3 (Third Quartile): The same as the 75th percentile, marking the cutoff for the top 25% of values.

Finding quartile values follows the same procedure as finding percentiles. For example, if the 25th percentile is calculated to be 25 minutes, Q1 is also 25 minutes.

Box Plots

Definition: Box Plot

A box plot is a visual representation of the five-number summary: minimum, Q1, median (Q2), Q3, and maximum. It helps identify the spread and potential outliers in the dataset.
