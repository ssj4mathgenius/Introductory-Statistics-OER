Probability Review Exercises

Example : Probability from a Contingency Table

A college health center recorded whether \(200\) students received a flu vaccine and whether they were diagnosed with the flu during the winter term. The results are shown in the contingency table below.

Student flu diagnoses and vaccination status for a sample of \(200\) students
Flu No Flu Totals
Vaccinated 18 102 120
Not Vaccinated 42 38 80
Totals 60 140 200

Use the table to find each probability below.

  1. Find the probability that a randomly selected student had the flu.
  2. Find the probability that a randomly selected student was vaccinated or had the flu.
  3. Find the probability that a randomly selected student was vaccinated and had the flu.
  4. If one student is selected at random, replaced, and then another student is selected, find the probability that both selected students had the flu.
  5. If two students are selected at random without replacement, find the probability that both selected students had the flu.
  6. Find the probability that a student had the flu given that the student was vaccinated.

Solution

  1. The probability of selecting a student who had the flu is

    \[ P(\text{Flu})=\frac{60}{200}=0.30 \]

  2. For an or probability, add the two individual probabilities and subtract the overlap:

    \[ P(\text{Vaccinated or Flu})=P(\text{Vaccinated})+P(\text{Flu})-P(\text{Vaccinated and Flu}) \] \[ =\frac{120}{200}+\frac{60}{200}-\frac{18}{200} =\frac{162}{200}=0.81 \]

  3. The overlap is the cell where both conditions happen together:

    \[ P(\text{Vaccinated and Flu})=\frac{18}{200}=0.09 \]

  4. With replacement, the probability stays the same on both draws:

    \[ P(\text{Flu twice with replacement})=\frac{60}{200}\cdot\frac{60}{200} =\frac{9}{100}=0.09 \]

  5. Without replacement, the second probability changes after the first selection:

    \[ P(\text{Flu twice without replacement})=\frac{60}{200}\cdot\frac{59}{199} =\frac{177}{1990}\approx 0.0889 \]

  6. A conditional probability uses the vaccinated row total as the new denominator:

    \[ P(\text{Flu}\mid \text{Vaccinated})=\frac{18}{120}=0.15 \]

Example : Finding an “At Least One” Probability

A quality-control manager randomly selects \(3\) light bulbs from a shipment of \(10\) bulbs. In the shipment, \(2\) bulbs are defective and \(8\) are not defective. If the bulbs are selected without replacement, find the probability that at least one of the selected bulbs is defective.

Solution

To find an at least one probability, it is usually easiest to subtract the probability of the complement from \(1\).

The complement of “at least one defective” is “no defective bulbs are selected.”

\[ P(\text{at least one defective})=1-P(\text{no defective}) \]

The probability of selecting \(3\) nondefective bulbs without replacement is

\[ P(\text{no defective})=\frac{8}{10}\cdot\frac{7}{9}\cdot\frac{6}{8} =\frac{7}{15} \]

So,

\[ P(\text{at least one defective})=1-\frac{7}{15}=\frac{8}{15}\approx 0.5333 \]

Therefore, the probability of selecting at least one defective bulb is \(\frac{8}{15}\), or about \(0.5333\).
